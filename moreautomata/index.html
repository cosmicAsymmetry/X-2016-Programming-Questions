Programming · X 2016

Welcome to Day 2 of the Programming Event of MINET X 2016. We hope you have as much fun solving the problems as we had making them. You have been provided with the Code::Blocks and Turbo C++ environments.

The event will be three hours long. There are five rounds and each round comprises of three questions. Your answers will have a direct bearing on your team’s stock prices. The scoring method followed is the same as yesterday.

Whenever you wish to attempt a question, raise your hand and one of us will come to you. Note that whenever there is an unsuccessful attempt, the maximum points for a problem are halved. Pitcher Problem

You are given two pitchers, one of which can accommodate $p$ liters of water and the other $q$ liters, with $p \leq q$. You have to tell if it is possible to obtain exactly $x$ liters of water in one of the vessels according to the instructions listed below. If it is possible, print $1$; if not, print $-1$. Initially, both pitchers are empty. The following operations can be done:

  • Emptying a vessel completely.
  • Filling a vessel completely
  • Pouring water from one vessel to the other without spilling, until one of the vessels is either full or empty.

Input: Three space-separated integers, $p$, $q$ and $x$.

Output: Print either $1$ or $-1$, as instructed in the problem statement.

Limits: $0 \leq p \leq q \leq 2^{31}-1$ and $2 < x \leq q$

Example

Input Output
3 7 2 1
Explanation: First, the 3 litre pitcher is filled. Then all the water of the 3 litre pitcher is poured into the 7 litre pitcher, which now contains 3 litre of water. This process is repeated again and the 7 litre pitcher now contains 6 litres. Again the 3 litre pitcher is filled with water and all the water is again poured into the 7 litre pitcher leaving 2 litres in the 3 litre pitcher.

Tank Trouble

A general decides to reward all $N$ of his cadets for doing well in their last exam. He places $N$ tanks in a base, and labels them $1$ to $N$. The cadets’ dog tags (again, labeled $1$ to $N$ for the corresponding cadet) are randomly shuffled and distributed among the tanks (one tag per tank).

Then, a game begins. The cadets will wait outside the base and—one by one—each cadet is allowed to go in and open up to $K$ tanks in search of his dog tag. The tags cannot be moved. After the cadet is done, he must close all the tanks and leave the base exactly as it was upon his entry. The cadets are not allowed to communicate with one another during the game.

If all $N$ cadets can find their dog tags, then they win; otherwise, they lose. Before starting the game, the cadets are given a moment to discuss a strategy for maximizing their chances of winning. No cadet has prior knowledge about the distribution of the dog tags.

Assuming optimal play, what is the probability that $N$ cadets will win the game if they are allowed to look at up to $K$ tanks?

Input: Two space-separated integers, $N$ and $K$.

Output: The required probability, correct to $10^{-7}$ precision.

Limit: $2 \leq K \leq N \leq 100000$

Example

Input Output
8 3 0.1467262

Round Pokémon·

Chanaz goes to catch em’ all

One day Chanaz got motivated to be the best, so she planned to catch Pokémon. She has to catch a total of $n$ Pokémon. But sadly, Chanaz is exhausted, so she works as follows :
First she catches $v$ Pokémon, takes rest at the Pokémon Centre , then she catches as many as $[v / k]$ Pokémon, takes rest again, then catches $[v / (k^2)]$ Pokémon and so on, forming the sequence :
$[v], [v / k], [v / k^2], [v / k^3], [v / k^4], [v / k^5], [v / k^6] ...$ The expression $[a / b]$ is regarded as the integral part from dividing number $a$ by number $b$, that is, the Greatest Integer Function(Floor function).

The moment the value of $[v / (k^p)]= 0$, Chanaz stops and goes to sleep. Once she sleeps, she gives up on catching Pokémon. But she wants to have $n$ Pokémon till then.

Chanaz is wondering, what minimum value $v$ can take to let her catch not less than n Pokémon before she falls asleep. Can you help her?

Input: The input consists of two integers $n$ and $k$, separated by spaces — the Number of pokemon she has to catch, and the productivity reduction coefficient.

Output: Print the minimum value of $v$ that lets Chanaz catch $n$ Pokemon before she goes off to sleep.

Limit: $1 < x \leq 10^9$ and $2 \leq k \leq 10$

Example

Input Output
7 2 4
59 9 54
Explanation: In the first sample the answer is $v$ = 4. Chanaz catches Pokémon as following: first 4 Pokémon, then 2, then 1, and then Chanaz falls asleep. Thus, she manages to catch 4 + 2 + 1 = 7 Pokémon in the night and complete the task.
In the second sample the answer is $v$ = 54. Chanaz catches Pokémon in the following portions: 54, 6. The total sum is 54 + 6 = 60, that's more than $n$ = 59.

Productivity of Johto

Champion of Johto region wants it to be the Best of all , thus wants to increase the productivity of the trainers by dividing the region into provinces.

  1. The map of Johto is a rectangular matrix of $M$ *$N$ size and each cell denotes a single city. Each cell of the matrix in the input depicts the population of a city.
  2. The Champion has decided that any connected region having exactly $K$ cities can form a province. A region is said to be connected if each of the city is adjacent to at least one other city along one of its edges. A city can be part of multiple provinces.

Now the Champion wants to know the productivity number ($P$) of his fellow trainers.
This number is defined to be the product of the populations of all the provinces.
The population of a province is the sum of the populations of all the cities in the province.
Your task is to find the productivity number of the kingdom. Since it can be quite large, output it $mod$ $10^9+7$.



Input: The first line of the input contains three space-separated integers: $M$,$ N$, and $ K$, denoting the number of rows in the map, the number of columns in the map and the number of cities in one province. Each of the following $M$ lines contain $N$ space-separated integers, denoting the populations of the cities.



Output: $P$, the productivity number of the region.

Limits:
  • $1 \leq N \leq 100$
  • $1 \leq M \leq 10$
  • $1 \leq K \leq min(10, N * M)$
  • $1 \leq A_{i,j} \leq 1000$


Example

Input Output
2 2 2
1 2
3 4 		504
Explanation
Here are all the provinces:
Province $1$, population $4$:
$1 2$
$3 4$
Province $2$, population $3$:
$1 2$
$3 4$
Province $3$, population $6$:
$1 2$
$3 4$
Province $4$, population $7$:
$1 2$
$3 4$
Since the productivity number is a product of all the populations, we get $4 * 3 * 6 * 7 = 504$.
